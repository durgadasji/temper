# Prior-art dossier for theme-suite (and the parametric deriver)

Scope. This dossier positions two things against the field: the shipped v1 system (five static semantic-token modes on one luminance ladder, described in `README.md`) and the v2 concept in `ingots/lib/palette.ts` (a runtime solver that derives a full palette from orthogonal end-user primitives: a luminance notch for tone, a color family, and a color-vision setting, with text contrast expressed as a fraction of the achievable budget against the background, absolute WCAG floors, a black or white clamp that emerges near mid-luminance, and staggered status luminance so status colors survive hue removal).

The task the dossier serves is truthful positioning. Every claim about an external system carries a source URL. The verdict section is deliberately adversarial toward our own novelty claims, because a README that overclaims is a README an expert can dismantle.

## Comparison table

Legend for cells: Yes, No, Partial, or a short qualifier. "Parametric generation" means colors are computed from inputs rather than hand-picked. "Contrast-guaranteed" means the system holds a contrast floor by construction. "Perceptual color space" means the math runs in a perceptually uniform space (CIELAB, LCH, OKLCH, or HCT) rather than sRGB or HSL. "Runtime user control" means the end reader, not only the designer, can move the inputs. "Orthogonal primitives" means the user-facing inputs are independent axes. "Vision-deficiency support" means color-vision deficiency is handled in generation, not only tested after the fact. "Text-first encoding" means information survives with hue removed. "Standards-signal integration" means the system maps onto the CSS user-preference machinery (`prefers-color-scheme`, `prefers-contrast`, `forced-colors`, `light-dark()`, `color-scheme`).

## Findings per system

Adobe Leonardo is the closest prior art to our deriver, and the closest to our headline claim. It generates color swatches from a target contrast ratio rather than from hand-picked hex, exactly inverting the usual pick-then-check workflow, and it is open source under Apache-2.0 ([leonardocolor.io](https://leonardocolor.io/), [github.com/adobe/leonardo](https://github.com/adobe/leonardo)). More significantly for us, Leonardo ships "adaptive themes" that expose brightness, contrast, and saturation as runtime controls to the end user, regenerating the entire UI palette live while holding the floors the designer configured ([github README](https://github.com/adobe/leonardo/blob/main/README.md), [InfoQ writeup](https://www.infoq.com/news/2020/03/adobe-leonardo-accessible-colors/)). Product teams already use it to offer accessibility settings where a user drags global contrast and the palette re-solves. Its contrast math is still WCAG 2 target ratios, and its color engine uses Chroma.js with CIECAM02 and HSLuv ordering rather than a fully perceptual solve ([Nate Baldwin, Leonardo intro](https://medium.com/@NateBaldwin/leonardo-an-open-source-contrast-based-color-generator-92d61b6521d2)). Its color-vision-deficiency handling is evaluation, a delta-E measurement of how far apart colors sit under each deficiency, not a generation mode that re-encodes status colors by luminance ([leonardocolor.io](https://leonardocolor.io/)).

Material Design 3 dynamic color is the most rigorous color engine in wide production. It converts a seed color into the HCT space (hue and chroma from CAM16, tone from CIELAB L\*), builds tonal palettes, and maps tones to roles such that a tone difference of 40 guarantees a WCAG ratio of at least 3.0 and a difference of 50 guarantees at least 4.5 ([m3.material.io, how the system works](https://m3.material.io/styles/color/system/how-the-system-works), [Material, science of color and design](https://m3.material.io/blog/science-of-color-design)). The utilities are open source under Apache-2.0 ([material-color-utilities](https://github.com/material-foundation/material-color-utilities)). The user control surface is a seed color (often extracted from wallpaper) plus, from Android 15, a system contrast level with medium and high steps that the theming engine folds into the palette ([Android Police, Android 15 contrast](https://www.androidpolice.com/android-15-beta-2-color-contrast-dynamic-material-you-themes/), [developer.android.com dynamic colors](https://developer.android.com/develop/ui/views/theming/dynamic-colors)). What it does not offer is an explicit orthogonal tone ladder chosen by the reader, or a color-vision generation mode.

Radix Colors is the reference for hand-crafted semantic scales. Each hue is a 12-step scale with steps assigned to fixed roles (backgrounds 1 to 2, component fills 3 to 5, borders 6 to 8, solids 9 to 10, text 11 to 12), and every scale ships a paired light and dark version ([radix-ui.com/colors, understanding the scale](https://www.radix-ui.com/colors/docs/palette-composition/understanding-the-scale)). Its contrast targets use APCA, and the two text steps are guaranteed to Lc 60 and Lc 90 on a step-2 background from the same scale ([radix-ui.com/colors](https://www.radix-ui.com/colors)). Radix is not parametric and has no runtime reader control; it is a fixed, expertly tuned set. It is relevant because our v1 token roles map almost one to one onto its role structure, so an expert will read our v1 as a Radix-shaped system with fewer hues and five tone modes instead of paired light and dark.

Huetone and Wildbit's Accessible Palette are the designer-time tools that already solved the perceptual-plus-contrast generation problem, and both went further than we did on color science. Huetone builds scales in OKLCH or CIELCH and targets APCA contrast ([huetone.ardov.me](https://huetone.ardov.me/), [github.com/ardov/huetone](https://github.com/ardov/huetone)). Wildbit's Accessible Palette generates palettes with consistent perceived lightness in CIELAB and recommends an APCA Lc 60 floor for readable text ([wildbit.com](https://www.wildbit.com/blog/accessible-palette-stop-using-hsl-for-color-systems)). Neither is a runtime end-user system; both are tools a designer uses to bake a palette. Their existence matters to our positioning because they are the source of the strongest single criticism of our deriver, below.

daisyUI's theme generator is the closest mass-market example of runtime, user-selectable, partly derived theming. It stores theme colors as CSS variables and, in its generator, derives optional colors (focus shades, readable content colors) from the few colors a user picks ([daisyui.com theme generator](https://daisyui.com/theme-generator/), [daisyui.com themes](https://daisyui.com/docs/themes/)). It is not contrast-floor-proven across every state, and until v5 its variables were HSL. It shows that runtime user theming with some derivation is a shipped, popular pattern, not a frontier.

The CSS platform primitives are the machinery a theming package should map onto rather than reinvent. `prefers-color-scheme` reports the OS light or dark choice; `prefers-contrast` reports more, less, or custom; `forced-colors` reports that the user has imposed a limited palette (Windows High Contrast is the canonical case, and it overrides author colors); `color-scheme` opts a page into native light or dark form controls and scrollbars; and `light-dark()` lets one declaration carry both values, shipping in Chrome 123, Safari 17.4, and Firefox 120 ([MDN prefers-color-scheme](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/@media/prefers-color-scheme), [MDN prefers-contrast](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/At-rules/@media/prefers-contrast), [MDN forced-colors](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/At-rules/@media/forced-colors), [CSS-Tricks color-scheme](https://css-tricks.com/almanac/properties/c/color-scheme/), [edge-cases.com light-dark()](https://www.edge-cases.com/css/css-light-dark-function)). Our v1 already sets `color-scheme` per mode and resolves System from `prefers-color-scheme`, which is the right instinct. We do not yet honor `prefers-contrast` or `forced-colors`, and an accessibility reviewer will expect a theming package to defer to `forced-colors` rather than fight it.

Accessibility overlay products (UserWay, accessiBe, and peers) are the cautionary tale for any reader-facing accessibility widget, and the lesson is specific, not vague. These products inject a script that promises WCAG compliance and legal safety through a floating widget ([Adrian Roselli, UserWay will get you sued](https://adrianroselli.com/2021/09/userway-will-get-you-sued.html)). The disability community opposes them because they mask rather than fix, they interfere with real assistive technology such as JAWS and NVDA, and blind developers have published guides to block them ([Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/accessibility-overlay-disabled-users-389394), [Lainey Feingold, overlays](https://www.lflegal.com/category/digital-accessibility/overlays/)). The National Federation of the Blind called overlay claims misleading and unproven, and roughly 600 accessibility practitioners signed the Overlay Fact Sheet asking sites to remove them ([Adirondack summary of NFB and Fact Sheet](https://adirondackwebsitedesign.com/lawsuits-rise-against-companies-using-overlay-solutions-like-accessibe-and-userway-adirondack-website-design/)). In April 2025 the FTC fined accessiBe one million dollars for misrepresenting its tool's ability to make sites WCAG compliant ([TestParty on the FTC action and lawsuit volume](https://testparty.ai/blog/why-800-businesses-with-accessibe-were-still-sued)). Over 800 businesses running overlays were sued in 2023 and 2024, at rates matching sites with no solution at all ([same TestParty analysis](https://testparty.ai/blog/why-800-businesses-with-accessibe-were-still-sued)). The lesson for our tuner is sharp: a reader-facing color widget must actually change the underlying tokens and floors, must not claim to be compliance in itself, and must never present itself as a substitute for building the page accessibly.

Flat theme lists are the incumbent UX our system implicitly argues against, and they are enormous. The VS Code marketplace lists thousands of color themes as a flat, searchable catalog; terminal collections are similar, with the mbadolato set alone shipping over 450 schemes and base16 defining a sixteen-color guideline with many downstream ports ([iterm2colorschemes.com](https://iterm2colorschemes.com/), [mbadolato/iTerm2-Color-Schemes](https://github.com/mbadolato/iterm2-color-schemes)). The point for positioning is not that these are bad; many are lovingly made. It is that a flat list does not compose. Two themes cannot be combined, a theme carries no contrast guarantee, adding a brand color means authoring a whole new theme by hand, and color-vision safety is left to the individual author. A parametric system trades the browseable variety of a flat list for composition and floors, and that is the honest tradeoff to name.

## Verdict: what is assembled, and what is not

The task asked for adversarial honesty, so here it is. Most of what our system does is assembled from existing ingredients, and several ingredients exist in more advanced form elsewhere.

Parametric generation from a target contrast is not novel. Adobe Leonardo is built on exactly this inversion, solving for the color that hits a target ratio rather than picking then checking ([leonardocolor.io](https://leonardocolor.io/)). Our binary search over lightness for a target ratio is a simpler implementation of the same idea.

Runtime, reader-facing control that re-solves the palette while holding floors is not novel either. Leonardo's adaptive themes already expose brightness, contrast, and saturation to end users and regenerate live within designer-set limits ([github.com/adobe/leonardo](https://github.com/adobe/leonardo/blob/main/README.md)), and Android 15 exposes contrast levels to users over Material's dynamic palette ([Android Police](https://www.androidpolice.com/android-15-beta-2-color-contrast-dynamic-material-you-themes/)). Our runtime tuner is in the same category as Leonardo's, and Leonardo shipped it years earlier.

Generating a palette from a single swappable color primitive is Material's seed model and daisyUI's picker model ([m3.material.io](https://m3.material.io/styles/color/system/how-the-system-works), [daisyui.com](https://daisyui.com/theme-generator/)). Our "color family" object is a seed pair (a neutral hue and an accent hue) by another name.

Contrast-guaranteed semantic scales with role-mapped steps are Radix, and Radix reaches its floors with APCA rather than WCAG 2 ([radix-ui.com/colors](https://www.radix-ui.com/colors)). Our v1 token model is a Radix-shaped role system.

What has no clean prior assembly, as far as this search found, is the specific composition of three things at once: color-vision deficiency treated as a first-class orthogonal generation axis (not a post-hoc test and not a display filter) inside a runtime reader-facing solver, where under a vision setting the status hues move onto axes the given vision can separate and take staggered luminance targets so success, warning, and danger remain distinguishable with hue removed entirely (the text-first encoding). Leonardo tests for color-vision deficiency but does not generate a deficiency-specific palette; OS color filters and tools like Color Oracle and Sim Daltonism simulate or filter but do not generate a text-first palette ([colororacle.org](https://colororacle.org/), [Sim Daltonism](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/sim-daltonism/id693112260?mt=12)); Material and Radix hold contrast but do not offer a reader-chosen color-vision mode that re-encodes status by darkness. The "contrast budget as a fraction of the achievable range, with a glare cap, so the black or white clamp emerges near mid-luminance instead of being a special case" is also a specific formulation I did not find named elsewhere; it is a refinement of Leonardo-style target-contrast generation rather than a new category, but the auto-scaling of the target to the background's available range is a real and defensible detail.

So the honest one-line verdict is this. The frame (solve a palette to contrast floors and let the reader move the inputs) is Leonardo's, already shipped and more mature on the runtime side. The color science is behind Radix, Huetone, Wildbit, and Material, all of which use perceptual spaces and, in several cases, APCA, while we use HSL and WCAG 2. The plausibly original contribution is the assembly: folding a color-vision axis into the runtime solver as an orthogonal reader primitive, with status colors re-encoded by staggered luminance so meaning survives hue removal. That assembly is worth claiming. A claim of inventing contrast-based or runtime theming is not.

## Positioning: sentences that survive expert scrutiny

The following candidate sentences are written to be defensible against the people cited above. Pick from them; do not use all four.

"theme-suite treats a palette as something you solve to accessibility floors rather than pick by hand, an approach pioneered by tools like Adobe Leonardo and Material Design 3, and extends it by making color-vision as a reader-selectable axis of the same solver."

"Where most contrast-aware color tools run at design time, theme-suite runs the solver at read time: the same engine that guarantees the floors also lets the reader move tone, color family, and color-vision mode, so accessibility is a property of the running page, not a check performed once before shipping."

"The distinctive move is text-first color-vision support: under a color-vision setting the solver re-encodes status colors by luminance so success, warning, and danger stay apart with hue removed entirely, rather than only testing an existing palette for deficiency, which is where Leonardo and most palettes stop."

"theme-suite ships a hand-tuned static baseline that needs no JavaScript and maps onto the CSS `color-scheme` and `prefers-color-scheme` signals, with the parametric solver as an optional layer on top, so a site gets guaranteed floors with progressive enhancement rather than a required runtime."

## Risks: the pushback an expert will give, and how to preempt it

The single biggest risk is the color science, and it has two parts. First, the deriver solves in HSL. HSL is a 1970s transform of RGB that ignores human perception, so equal HSL lightness values look dramatically unequal (pure blue reads far darker than pure yellow at the same L), which is precisely why the field moved to CIELAB, LCH, OKLCH, and HCT ([Wildbit, stop using HSL for color systems](https://www.wildbit.com/blog/accessible-palette-stop-using-hsl-for-color-systems)). Second, the contrast target is WCAG 2 relative luminance, and WCAG 2 contrast is widely criticized as inaccurate, with dark themes as its worst case: a pair that passes 4.5 to 1 on paper can read as muddy or unreadable when one color is near black, which is the motivation for APCA ([66colorful, APCA vs WCAG 2](https://66colorful.com/blog/apca-contrast/), [Capellic, WCAG to APCA](https://capellic.com/insights/accessible-colors)). An expert will note that Radix, Huetone, Wildbit, and Material all use perceptual spaces and that Radix and the two tools use APCA, so our solver is built on the two color primitives the field is actively trying to retire. Preempt this by stating plainly that the deriver's architecture is metric-agnostic (it binary-searches a monotone lightness axis against a target ratio, and nothing in that loop depends on the space being HSL or the metric being WCAG 2), that HSL and WCAG 2 are the current defaults chosen for zero dependencies and legal defensibility, and that OKLCH plus APCA is the intended upgrade path. Do not claim state-of-the-art color science; claim a defensible default with a clear upgrade path.

The second risk is the WCAG 2 versus APCA compliance trap, and it cuts both ways. Choosing WCAG 2 is defensible because WCAG 2 is the standard actually required by law and policy, while APCA was removed from the July 2023 WCAG 3 working draft, is described by its own author as draft and not yet WCAG 3, and cannot be used to claim conformance ([Adrian Roselli, WCAG 3 contrast status](https://adrianroselli.com/2026/04/wcag3-contrast-as-of-april-2026.html), [Eric Eggert, WCAG 3 is not ready](https://yatil.net/blog/wcag-3-is-not-ready-yet)). The APCA-W3 package is licensed specifically to the W3C and AGWG with limitations and exclusions, and its documentation warns against claiming WCAG compliance from it ([github.com/Myndex/apca-w3](https://github.com/Myndex/apca-w3)). So the preemption is to frame WCAG 2 as the compliance floor we guarantee and APCA as a perceptual refinement we can add for quality, never to imply either that WCAG 2 is state of the art or that APCA is a legal standard. Owning both halves of this is stronger than picking a side.

The third risk is the limits of color-vision handling. Deficiency simulations are approximations, individual perception varies within the same deficiency type, and no generated palette makes hue-coded information safe on its own ([sgt-lab, WCAG and color blindness](https://sgt-lab.com/blog/color-blindness-W3C-guidelines/), [W3C, use of color 1.4.1](https://www.w3.org/TR/UNDERSTANDING-WCAG20/visual-audio-contrast-without-color.html)). Our staggered-luminance encoding is a real improvement, since luminance separation survives when hue does not, but it is not a substitute for redundant non-color cues such as icons, labels, and shapes, which WCAG 1.4.1 requires regardless of palette. Preempt by scoping the claim: the vision settings make the palette itself more robust to deficiency, and they explicitly do not remove the obligation to encode meaning redundantly in text and shape. Overclaiming here invites the overlay comparison.

The fourth risk is the overlay-widget stigma attaching to the runtime tuner. Because our tuner is a reader-facing accessibility control, a reviewer primed by UserWay and accessiBe may pattern-match it to an overlay. The defense is to be concrete about the difference: the tuner writes real CSS custom properties that the whole page already consumes, it does not inject a post-hoc DOM filter over an unchanged page, it does not intercept or replace assistive technology, it defers to the OS through `color-scheme` and `prefers-color-scheme` rather than overriding the user's system choices, and it never claims to be compliance in itself ([lessons from the overlay opposition](https://www.lflegal.com/category/digital-accessibility/overlays/), [FTC accessiBe action](https://testparty.ai/blog/why-800-businesses-with-accessibe-were-still-sued)). State this proactively in the README so the comparison is answered before it is raised.

The fifth risk is smaller but worth a sentence: we do not yet integrate `prefers-contrast` or `forced-colors`. An accessibility reviewer will expect a serious theming package to raise contrast when the user asks for `prefers-contrast: more` and to step out of the way under `forced-colors` (Windows High Contrast), which overrides author colors by design ([MDN forced-colors](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/At-rules/@media/forced-colors), [MDN prefers-contrast](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/At-rules/@media/prefers-contrast)). Either wire these up or name them as known gaps; do not let a reviewer discover them.
